Plain-English Medical Explainer

What is sickle cell disease?

Sickle cell disease is an inherited blood disorder. It changes the shape and behavior of red blood cells, making it harder for blood to carry oxygen smoothly through the body. This can cause pain, anemia, infections, organ damage, and other serious complications.

The simplest explanation

Think of red blood cells like delivery trucks carrying oxygen. In sickle cell disease, many of those “trucks” become stiff and curved instead of soft and round. Because they are stiff, they can break down early and get stuck in tiny blood vessels. When that happens, parts of the body may not get enough oxygen, which can cause severe pain and damage over time.

What it is

A genetic condition

A person is born with sickle cell disease after inheriting abnormal hemoglobin genes from both parents. The most common severe type is HbSS, often called sickle cell anemia.

The blood problem

Red cells change shape

Normal red blood cells are flexible and round. Sickle cells become hard, sticky, and C-shaped, so they can block small vessels and die too early.

The body effect

Oxygen delivery suffers

When cells block blood flow or die early, patients can develop anemia, pain episodes, fatigue, infections, strokes, acute chest syndrome, and organ problems.

The care goal

Prevent complications

Treatment focuses on preventing pain crises, treating pain quickly, reducing infections, monitoring organs, and using medicines or advanced therapies when appropriate.

How do you diagnose it?

1
Blood test. Sickle cell disease is diagnosed with a blood test that identifies the type of hemoglobin a person has.
2
Newborn screening. In the United States, most cases are found at birth through routine newborn screening before symptoms begin.
3
Confirmatory testing. If a screen is positive, doctors confirm the diagnosis and usually connect the child with a hematologist, a blood specialist.
4
Before birth. Prenatal testing, such as chorionic villus sampling or amniocentesis, can diagnose sickle cell disease before delivery in some pregnancies.
5
Family testing and counseling. Parents may be tested for sickle cell trait to understand inheritance risk for future children.

What does it affect clinically?

  • Pain crises: blocked blood flow can cause sudden, severe pain episodes.
  • Anemia: sickle cells die early, creating a chronic shortage of red blood cells.
  • Infections: children can be at higher risk, especially from certain bacteria.
  • Acute chest syndrome: a serious lung complication with chest pain, fever, cough, or breathing trouble.
  • Stroke risk: blood vessel problems can increase stroke risk, especially in children with severe disease.
  • Organ damage: repeated low oxygen and blocked flow can affect the spleen, kidneys, eyes, bones, lungs, and brain.
  • Daily life: fatigue, missed school/work, hospital visits, chronic pain, and emotional stress can become major burdens.

How do you treat it?

  • Routine specialist care: regular hematology follow-up and monitoring.
  • Vaccines and antibiotics: prevention of dangerous infections; young children with HbSS often receive daily penicillin until at least age 5.
  • Hydroxyurea: a common oral medicine that can reduce sickling, pain crises, hospitalizations, and some complications.
  • Pain treatment: hydration, heat, over-the-counter pain medicine for mild pain, and prescription or hospital-based treatment for severe pain.
  • Blood transfusions: used for severe anemia, stroke prevention in selected patients, acute chest syndrome, or other complications.
  • Screening: stroke screening with transcranial Doppler in children, eye exams, and organ monitoring.
  • Curative or transformative options: bone marrow/stem cell transplant and newer gene therapies may be options for some patients.

Important note for a normal person

Sickle cell disease is not “just pain.” It is a lifelong blood disorder that can affect nearly every organ system. The good news is that early diagnosis, preventive care, medicines, careful monitoring, and advanced therapies can reduce complications and help many patients live longer, fuller lives.
